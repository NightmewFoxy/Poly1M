# Academic / Quant-Catalog Sweep — Crypto Trading Strategies

**Date:** 2026-07-17
**Scope:** arXiv q-fin, SSRN, ScienceDirect/Emerald/Springer journals, QuantPedia, academic-adjacent quant blogs (Concretum Group, paperswithbacktest, Quantocracy/StratProof).
**Filter applied:** retail trader, $500–$5,000, Windows home PC in Malaysia, retail latency (no colocation/HFT). Read-only research only, no orders placed.
**Excluded per instructions (already dead):** Polymarket binary-merge arb, Polymarket LP rewards, Polymarket copy-trading, esports betting, pump.fun sniping.

**Overall editorial note:** this sweep is deliberately skeptical. Crypto academic literature is full of gross-of-fee, in-sample, or survivorship-tainted results. Every entry below states explicitly whether fees/slippage were modeled, and flags decay where the evidence shows it. Two sources stood out for rigor and are ranked highest *because* of methodology, not because they show the biggest numbers: an arXiv walk-forward ML paper that transparently shows an edge collapsing under realistic costs, and a real-fee 10-day forward test that killed 16 of 22 popular retail strategies.

## Tier 1 — Strongest candidates for tonight's verification pass

### 1. Dynamic Grid Trading (DGT) with regime detection
- **Mechanism:** Standard grid trading (place a ladder of buy/sell limit orders at fixed price intervals around current price, profiting from round-trips as price oscillates) — but the "dynamic" version adds a regime-detection layer that **resets/re-centers the grid and adjusts its spacing** when the market shifts from range-bound to trending, instead of blindly averaging down/up through a trend like a plain grid does.
- **Claimed performance:** Plain grid: "near-zero expected return before fees," **negative after fees**. DGT: **annualized IRR 60–70%** on BTC and ETH; cuts ETH's max drawdown to **-50% vs -80%** for buy-and-hold over the same window. Source: Chen, Chen & Jang, "Dynamic Grid Trading Strategy: From Zero Expectation to Market Outperformance," **arXiv:2506.11921** (q-fin.TR, submitted June 2025). Sample period **Jan 2021–Jul 2024**, minute-level data.
- **Gross/net of fees:** Ambiguous in secondary summaries — the paper explicitly proves the *plain* grid's zero-expectation result is pre-fee and goes negative post-fee, but it is not confirmed from the abstract alone whether the headline 60–70% DGT IRR is net of trading fees. **This must be checked in the full PDF before trusting the number.**
- **Data to reproduce tonight:** BTC/USDT and ETH/USDT 1-minute klines from `data.binance.vision` (free, exactly matches the paper's granularity and period, which is fully within Binance's historical archive).
- **Capital fit / kill factors:** Excellent fit — spot-only, no leverage or shorting required, works fine fragmented into many small grid-level orders at $1–5k (Binance minimum notional ~$10/order). Kill risk: grid trading's known Achilles' heel is a sustained one-way trend blowing through the top/bottom of the grid; the paper's own "plain grid" result shows this failure mode empirically. Must verify the regime-detection logic doesn't just curve-fit the 2021–2024 window (one bull run + one bear crash + one chop phase — suspiciously convenient regime coverage for a 3.5-year sample).

### 2. ML-based BTC forecasting with cost-aware execution filter (XGBoost/LSTM/iTransformer)
- **Mechanism:** Three model families (XGBoost, LSTM, iTransformer) forecast next-hour BTC log-return from lagged OHLCV + technical indicators (+ EGARCH volatility in one variant). A **cost-aware execution filter** only changes position when the forecast magnitude exceeds `λ × transaction_cost` (λ=2.0), which cuts turnover from ~10,600 trades to as few as **197–251 trades** over the whole test span — directly targeting the standard failure mode of short-horizon signals (fees eating the edge via over-trading).
- **Claimed performance:** Gross (0 fees), long-only: XGBoost ARC 73.5%/Sharpe 1.27; iTransformer ARC 129%/Sharpe 2.59. **Net of 10bps costs, naive sign-rule: all models collapse to strongly negative** (XGBoost -64% return/-1.25 Sharpe). **Net of costs WITH the cost-aware filter, best config (XGBoost, OHLCV+technicals, long-only): ARC 65.4%, Sharpe 1.09, max drawdown -67.4%**, vs. buy-and-hold's 56.3% return / 0.82 Sharpe over the same span. Source: **arXiv:2606.00060**, "Machine Learning-Based Bitcoin Trading Under Transaction Costs: Evidence From Walk-Forward Forecasting" (2026). Sample **1 Dec 2017 – 1 Jan 2026**, hourly Binance BTC/USDT futures, 27-fold walk-forward (12mo train / 3mo validate / 3mo test, rolled every 3 months).
- **Gross/net of fees:** Explicitly both, cleanly separated — this is the single most fee-transparent paper found in this sweep.
- **The honest catch:** after Holm correction for multiple testing (circular block-bootstrap, 10,000 reps), **none of the cost-aware strategies statistically beat buy-and-hold on a Sharpe basis.** The authors are not selling an edge; they're demonstrating how hard it is to find one net of realistic costs. Worth citing for that honesty alone, and worth testing tonight because it needs zero exotic data.
- **Data to reproduce tonight:** Pure hourly OHLCV, BTC/USDT — `data.binance.vision`, no order-book/on-chain/sentiment/funding data needed at all (paper explicitly disclaims using any of that). This is the easiest reproduction in the whole sweep.
- **Capital fit / kill factors:** Turnover this low (~250 trades / 8 years) is trivially affordable at $1–5k. Kill factor: building and *maintaining* this (EGARCH refits, Optuna hyperparameter search every 3-month roll) is real engineering overhead for a strategy that, per the authors' own significance testing, may not beat simply holding BTC.

### 3. Crypto trend-following via Donchian-channel ensemble, rotational top-20 portfolio
- **Mechanism:** Ensemble of Donchian-channel breakout signals at multiple lookback horizons, combined into a single trend score per coin; volatility-based position sizing (inverse-vol weighting); portfolio rotates across the **top 20 most liquid cryptocurrencies**, going long/flat (long-short variants also tested per the authors' other papers) based on ensemble trend direction.
- **Claimed performance:** **Sharpe ratio above 1.5**, **annualized alpha of 10.8% vs. Bitcoin**, described by the authors as net-of-fees. Source: Carlo Zarattini, Alberto Pagani, Andrea Barbon, **"Catching Crypto Trends; A Tactical Approach for Bitcoin and Altcoins,"** SSRN 5209907 / Swiss Finance Institute Research Paper (April 2025). https://papers.ssrn.com/sol3/papers.cfm?abstract_id=5209907 — also summarized at https://concretumgroup.com/catching-crypto-trends-a-tactical-approach-for-bitcoin-and-altcoins/. 5,700+ SSRN downloads, authors are practitioner-quants (Concretum Group) with a track record of well-regarded, methodologically careful trend papers (they also published the companion "Seasonality in Bitcoin Intraday Trend Trading").
- **Gross/net of fees:** Authors state net-of-fees, but exact fee assumption, rebalance frequency, CAGR and max drawdown are **not visible without the full PDF** — the public summary omits these. Flag as "claimed net" pending verification.
- **Data to reproduce tonight:** Daily OHLCV for the top ~20 liquid coins — `data.binance.vision` daily klines, or CoinGecko free API for a broader universe/market-cap ranking history.
- **Capital fit / kill factors:** Good fit at $1–5k (Binance minimum notional ~$10 makes 20-way fractional positions workable), no shorting/derivatives required for the long-only version. Kill risk: rotating across 20 names means fee/spread drag scales with rebalance frequency — needs the exact rebalance cadence (not disclosed in free summary) to judge whether $1-5k survives the round-trip costs of 20 simultaneous small positions.

### 4. BTC time-of-day seasonality + N-day-high trend continuation
- **Mechanism (seasonality leg):** Buy BTC at **21:00 UTC**, sell at **23:00 UTC**, every day — a fixed 2-hour intraday long, flat otherwise. **Mechanism (trend leg, "MAX strategy"):** buy when price makes a new N-day high (best N=10 in testing), a simple momentum-continuation filter re-verified out of sample.
- **Claimed performance:** Seasonality trade: **~33% annualized return**, "dramatically lower volatility and drawdown" than buy-and-hold (exact Sharpe/max-DD not in the free excerpt). MAX (10-day-high) trend strategy: "higher returns with lower drawdown than buy-and-hold," remained effective in the **2022–2024 out-of-sample stress test** (including through the BTC decline), while the mirror-image MIN (buy-the-dip) strategy did not hold up out of sample. Sources: Matus Padysak & Radovan Vojtko, **"Seasonality, Trend-following, and Mean Reversion in Bitcoin"** (2022) and the follow-up **"Revisiting Trend-following and Mean-reversion Strategies in Bitcoin"** (Beluska & Vojtko, SSRN 4955617), both summarized/hosted at QuantPedia (https://quantpedia.com/revisiting-trend-following-and-mean-reversion-strategies-in-bitcoin/) and https://blog.paperswithbacktest.com/p/bitcoin-never-sleeps-exploiting-seasonality. Full sample **Oct 2015 – Aug 2024**, Gemini hourly data via CryptoDataDownload.
- **Gross/net of fees:** **Gross** — no transaction costs mentioned in either the original or the revisit. This matters less for the seasonality leg (one round-trip per day) than it would for a higher-frequency strategy, but still needs a fee pass before trusting the 33% number.
- **Data to reproduce tonight:** Hourly BTC OHLCV — `data.binance.vision` 1h klines cover the full period trivially (Binance launched well before 2015... actually Binance itself launched 2017, so for the pre-2017 portion use Gemini/Kraken historical hourly data, both free; post-2017 use Binance).
- **Capital fit / kill factors:** Best capital fit in the whole sweep — single coin, spot-only, one trade per day, no fragmentation. Kill factor: the seasonality "edge" is a classic calendar-anomaly claim (like the old CME-open effects in FX) — worth extra scrutiny for whether it's a UTC-timezone artifact of when a particular subset of institutional flow used to trade, which can decay silently once documented (Goodhart's law risk, and QuantPedia's own framing calls this "revisiting" for a reason).

## Tier 2 — Credible but flagged (decay, credibility split, or narrow applicability)

### 5. Short-horizon cross-sectional momentum (1–4 week) vs. longer-horizon reversal
- **Mechanism:** Rank a broad universe of coins by trailing return over a formation window; go long past winners / short past losers, hold for a matching period. Crypto's "faster metabolism" means the standard equity playbook compresses: **momentum works at 1–4 week formation/holding**, then **flips to reversal beyond ~1 month** (much faster switch than equities' 3–12 month momentum window).
- **Claimed performance:** Victoria Dobrynskaya, **"Cryptocurrency Momentum and Reversal,"** Journal of Alternative Investments, Summer 2023 (SSRN 3913263, https://papers.ssrn.com/sol3/papers.cfm?abstract_id=3913263): sample of the **2,000 largest cryptocurrencies, 2014–2020**; reversal strategy Sharpe ratio of **4.58 annualized** (momentum and reversal both "not exposed to standard crypto risk factors"). Separately, Han/Kang/Ryu, **"Time-Series and Cross-Sectional Momentum in the Cryptocurrency Market: A Comprehensive Analysis under Realistic Assumptions,"** SSRN 4675565 (https://papers.ssrn.com/sol3/papers.cfm?abstract_id=4675565) explicitly re-tests momentum under realistic transaction-cost/capacity/shorting-constraint assumptions (paper itself paywalled beyond abstract at fetch time — **flag for manual retrieval**, since "realistic assumptions" is exactly the fee-aware version we want).
- **Gross/net of fees:** Dobrynskaya's headline Sharpe 4.58 appears to be from a **2,000-coin universe including illiquid microcaps** — this is the same population where the "Up or Down" paper (below) shows reversal effects are a liquidity-illusion. Treat the 4.58 figure with real suspicion until confirmed on a liquid-only subset.
- **Decay flag:** Sample ends **2020** — five years stale relative to today; crypto cross-sectional efficiency has plausibly improved since (more market-making bots, more liquid alt derivatives).
- **Data to reproduce tonight:** Daily closes across a broad coin universe — CoinGecko free API (`/coins/markets`, historical) for cross-sectional ranking; restrict to top-100-by-volume to sidestep the illiquidity issue.
- **Capital fit / kill factors:** Long-only momentum is easy at $1–5k; the long-short/reversal leg needs short access (available via Binance/Bybit perps even on small accounts) — adds margin complexity. Kill factor: if the edge is concentrated in micro-caps (plausible given the reversal literature below), spread/slippage at $1-5k retail execution likely erases most of the paper Sharpe.

### 6. Risk-managed momentum overlay (Barroso–Santa-Clara adaptation)
- **Mechanism:** Standard cross-sectional momentum, but position size is scaled inversely to trailing realized volatility of the momentum portfolio itself (not the individual assets) — classic vol-targeting overlay, adapted from Barroso & Santa-Clara (2015)'s equity momentum-crash fix.
- **Claimed performance:** Average weekly returns improve from **3.18% to 3.47%**; annualized Sharpe from **1.12 to 1.42**. Authors note the mechanism differs from equities: in crypto the gain comes from **return enhancement, not crash protection**, because crypto lacks equities' extended momentum-crash pattern. Source: ScienceDirect, *Finance Research Letters*, July 2025, "Cryptocurrency market risk-managed momentum strategies," https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/abs/pii/S1544612325011377. Authors state robustness checks include **transaction costs and short-sale constraints**.
- **Gross/net of fees:** Claimed robust to transaction costs per the abstract, but the exact fee assumption wasn't visible without full-text access (paywalled).
- **Data to reproduce tonight:** Same as #5 — daily cross-sectional coin data, add a rolling realized-vol calc on the momentum leg's own return series.
- **Capital fit / kill factors:** Same profile as #5 (needs a multi-coin basket + vol-scaling logic); reasonably serious peer-reviewed journal (Finance Research Letters) adds credibility over pure SSRN preprints.

### 7. Short-timeframe RSI/Bollinger-Band mean reversion — real-fee 10-day forward test
- **Mechanism:** Mean-reversion entries triggered by RSI extremes (optionally combined with Bollinger Band touches, volatility filters, or order-flow confirmation), tested across 5m/15m/30m/1H/4H/1D timeframes on 10 liquid coins (BTC, ETH, SOL, BNB, XRP, DOGE, ZEC, ADA, SUI, PEPE, TAO).
- **Claimed performance:** Of **22 popular retail strategies** paper-traded on **real Binance fees for 10 days** (26,765 total trades), **16 lost money** (average -0.078%/trade, cumulative -2,081%, ending $1,000 → $662.90). The **6 survivors were all RSI-based mean-reversion variants**; every trend-following strategy tested (MACD, Supertrend, EMA-cross) lost money net of fees. Top survivor (Bollinger Band + RSI): 1,018 trades, 53.8% win rate, **+0.188% average per trade**, +191% cumulative (summed, not compounded). Source: StratProof, "I paper-traded 22 popular crypto strategies on real fees for 10 days," https://stratproof.com/blog/paper-trading-22-strategies-real-fees (April 2026), featured on **Quantocracy**.
- **Gross/net of fees:** Explicitly **net of real fees** — Binance VIP1 maker 0.075%/taker 0.1%, plus 0.05–0.15% realized L2 spread modeled, ~0.25–0.30% round-trip total. This is the most fee-honest source in the sweep, and it used walk-forward train/test plus a **Deflated Sharpe Ratio correction (Bailey–López de Prado 2014)** for multi-testing across the 22 strategies.
- **Major caveat:** the forward-test window is only **10 days**. That's nowhere near enough to distinguish genuine edge from noise even with the Deflated Sharpe adjustment — treat this as "mean-reversion has better odds than trend-following net of fees at these timeframes" directional evidence, not proof of a standalone tradeable edge. Worth re-running over a longer window before trusting it.
- **Data to reproduce tonight:** Exactly reproducible — Binance OHLCV at 5m/15m/1h/4h/1D via `data.binance.vision`, same 10 coins.
- **Capital fit / kill factors:** Good fit ($1–5k, spot, no leverage needed). Kill factor: short-timeframe strategies need either bot automation (24/7 monitoring) or you miss most signals — not a "check once a day" strategy.

### 8. Perpetual funding-rate carry trade (short perp / long spot) — **DECAYED, included as a warning**
- **Mechanism:** Short a perpetual future, hold an equal-notional long spot position; collect the funding-rate payments (typically every 8h) as the perp trades at a premium to spot in bull-biased markets, while the spot/perp price delta is fully hedged (delta-neutral).
- **Claimed performance:** Annualized Sharpe ratio of **6.45 over the full 2020–2025 sample**, but **falling to 4.06 starting 2024, and turning negative in 2025**. Mean funding return was ~8%/year with 0.8% volatility over the full sample, but that premium has compressed. Source: **"Cryptocurrency as an Investable Asset Class: Coming of Age,"** arXiv:2510.14435 (Oct 2025), citing Schmeling, Schrimpf & Todorov (2023)'s methodology. https://arxiv.org/html/2510.14435v2.
- **Gross/net of fees:** Not fully specified, and moot given the sign flip.
- **Why it's dead:** Same underlying mechanism the project already proved dead for Polymarket binary-merge arb (see this repo's `HANDOFF.md`/`arb_fee_wall.md` memory) — **fee-walling and crowding compress a mechanically-guaranteed-looking edge to zero or negative once enough capital chases it.** This is the crypto-perp analogue and the timeline (negative by 2025) matches the general pattern of funding-rate carry getting arbed away as institutional players (and DeFi funding-rate vaults marketed to retail) piled in through 2023–2024.
- **Data to reproduce tonight:** Binance/Bybit funding-rate history (free via REST API, e.g., Binance `fapi/v1/fundingRate`) + spot price — trivial to confirm current-state funding is indeed thin/negative before wasting more time on this family.
- **Verdict:** Do not pursue as a standalone strategy. Worth a 15-minute tonight check of current funding rates only to confirm the decay is still true (it might mean-revert positive again briefly around specific catalysts — but that's event-driven opportunism, not a standing strategy).

### 9. Cross-exchange / cross-asset funding-rate arbitrage (dispersion trade)
- **Mechanism:** Instead of one coin's funding rate over time, compare funding rates for the **same coin across multiple venues simultaneously** (CEX: Binance, Bitmex; DEX: ApolloX, Drift) and across multiple coins (BTC, ETH, XRP, BNB, SOL) at once, rotating capital toward whichever venue/coin pair offers the fattest funding spread while staying delta-neutral.
- **Claimed performance:** Across **60 arbitrage scenarios**, returns of **up to 115.9% over 6 months** with max drawdown as low as **1.92%**. Source: "Exploring Risk and Return Profiles of Funding Rate Arbitrage on CEX and DEX," ScienceDirect, https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/pii/S2096720925000818 (2025; full text paywalled, abstract-level detail only obtained via secondary summaries/ResearchGate).
- **Gross/net of fees:** Unknown — could not access full text (403 on both ScienceDirect and ResearchGate mirrors).
- **Read with caution:** "up to 115.9%" strongly suggests this is the **best of 60 scenarios**, not a typical/average result — classic cherry-picking risk in how the headline number gets repeated across secondary sources. Needs the actual paper (or a SciHub-free institutional-access route) before treating the number as real.
- **Data to reproduce tonight:** Funding-rate histories across Binance, Bybit, OKX, dYdX, Drift (most publish free historical funding via REST API or aggregators like Coinglass) — moderately more setup than single-exchange strategies but fully doable with free data.
- **Capital fit / kill factors:** Running this properly needs accounts, KYC, and collateral spread across multiple CEXs and at least one DEX (Solana-based, so wallet + gas management) — meaningfully more operational overhead than a single-venue strategy for a $1–5k account, and collateral fragmentation across venues eats into capital efficiency at this size.

### 10. BTC options short-volatility / variance risk premium (VRP) harvesting
- **Mechanism:** Implied volatility on Deribit BTC options systematically exceeds subsequently realized volatility (negative variance risk premium), which in principle rewards **systematically selling options** (e.g., short straddles/strangles, collected as premium) rather than buying them.
- **Claimed performance:** Variance risk premium averages **~14% annualized** (12% in high-vol regimes, 17% in low-vol regimes); Bitcoin return premium ~66% annualized over the same decomposition; **Sharpe ratio ~0.8** cited for the overall risk-premium harvesting concept — "nearly twice the S&P 500's ~0.5." Source: Almeida, Grith & Miftachov, **"Risk Premia in the Bitcoin Market,"** arXiv:2410.15195 (2024), https://arxiv.org/html/2410.15195v2. Sample **Jul 2017 – Dec 2022**, Deribit data (1,992 days, ~5.4M transactions).
- **Gross/net of fees:** This is a **risk-premium decomposition paper, not an executable strategy backtest** — it does not simulate an actual short-vol trading strategy's P&L, position sizing, or margin, and explicitly does not net out costs (notes Deribit's 0.03% fee exists but doesn't apply it to a strategy). Treat the Sharpe-0.8 figure as a rough theoretical upper bound, not a validated backtest result.
- **Data to reproduce tonight:** Partial — Deribit does publish a free public API for options order books/trades and the DVOL implied-vol index; realized vol is computable from free spot data. Full historical options chain reconstruction is more work than the other candidates here.
- **Capital fit / kill factors:** Deribit accepts small accounts and portfolio margining, so $1–5k can technically run a small short-vol position — but **naked short options carry tail risk that can exceed account equity** (general short-vol literature cites drawdowns of multiples of the account in extreme scenarios). At this account size, only defined-risk structures (credit spreads, not naked strangles) are prudent, which caps the harvestable premium well below the theoretical number.

### 11. BTC–ETH cointegration pairs trading / statistical arbitrage
- **Mechanism:** Test BTC and ETH (or a wider basket incl. LTC/BCH) for cointegration (Engle-Granger/Johansen tests), construct a mean-reverting spread, and trade z-score deviations from the cointegrating relationship — long the underperformer/short the outperformer when the spread widens, unwind on reversion.
- **Claimed performance:** Wide range across sources of varying quality: a credible peer-reviewed source (Leung & Nguyen, **"Constructing Cointegrated Cryptocurrency Portfolios for Statistical Arbitrage,"** *Studies in Economics and Finance* (Emerald), https://www.emerald.com/sef/article/36/4/581/353687/) reports an average annual **Sharpe ratio of 1.53 per pair** with a **median max drawdown of 29%**. A separate, much-lower-credibility source (International Journal of Scientific Research and Archives, a pay-to-publish outlet — treat as low quality) claims BTC-ETH specifically achieved **Sharpe 2.45** (16.34% annualized return, 8.45% volatility). Other secondary citations mention Sharpe up to 3.97 with 7.94% max drawdown for a train/test-split variant, source quality unverified.
- **Gross/net of fees:** Not established for any of these — full text access failed (403s) on all attempts.
- **Decay flag:** The credible Emerald paper's data underlies a **~2015–2018-era** crypto market (paper published 2019) — a much less efficient market than today's, with far fewer market makers and arb bots active. BTC-ETH correlation/cointegration stability has been extensively arbitraged since institutional entry post-2020; treat the Sharpe 1.53+ figures as likely **significantly decayed**.
- **Data to reproduce tonight:** Trivial — daily/hourly BTC & ETH OHLCV from `data.binance.vision`, run an Engle-Granger test yourself.
- **Capital fit / kill factors:** Good fit at $1–5k (two-asset spread trade, no fragmentation), needs short access on one leg (available via Binance/Bybit perps). Main kill risk is the decay flag above, not capital or mechanics.

## Tier 3 — Included for completeness, but most likely kills

### 12. Exchange listing-announcement drift ("Binance effect")
- **Mechanism:** Buy a token immediately on announcement of a major-exchange (esp. Binance) listing, ride the announcement-driven pop, sell within days.
- **Claimed performance:** Academic event studies find average abnormal returns of **5.7% on listing day, 9.2%** in the ±3-day window generally across exchanges; **Binance-specific cumulative abnormal return of 17.5%** in some studies, with some exchanges showing listing-day pops up to **25.5%**. Sources: "Market Reaction to Exchange Listings of Cryptocurrencies" (Blockchain Research Lab working paper, ResearchGate) and related coverage.
- **Gross/net of fees:** Gross — these are academic abnormal-return event studies, not fee-aware trading backtests.
- **Decay flag:** A dedicated piece, **"How The Crypto's Biggest Listing Signal Reversed"** (ourcryptotalk.com), specifically documents this effect weakening/reversing in more recent periods as the pattern became crowded and front-run by bots watching announcement feeds in real time.
- **Kill factors:** Retail latency is the direct problem here — by the time a human retail trader in Malaysia sees a Binance listing tweet and manually places an order, algorithmic front-runners (who explicitly excluded from this mission's scope, but who are the competition here) have usually already captured the bulk of the move. Capacity is also inherently tiny (one-off events, thin order books on brand-new listings).

### 13. Stablecoin depeg mean-reversion
- **Mechanism:** When a major stablecoin (USDC, USDT, DAI) trades meaningfully below its $1 peg during a stress event, buy the discounted stablecoin expecting reversion to $1 as arbitrageurs/redemption mechanisms restore the peg.
- **Claimed performance:** Referenced in "Stable Unstable: Evaluating Mean Reversion Profitability in Stablecoin Pair Depeg Events" (ResearchGate, 2023) — profitability confirmed directionally but exact Sharpe/return figures weren't accessible from the abstract-level search.
- **Gross/net of fees:** Unclear, not verified.
- **Kill factors:** Extremely **low frequency** (USDC's March 2023 SVB depeg is the standout example in the last several years) — not a standing strategy, more an opportunistic "have dry powder ready" playbook. Direct $1 redemption with the issuer (Circle, Tether) is typically gated to whitelisted institutional accounts with minimum sizes far above $1–5k retail scale, so the retail version of this trade relies on the market repegging on its own, which is a weaker/slower version of the "true" arb.

### 14. Cross-venue/cross-country spot arbitrage (triangular arb, Kimchi-premium-style)
- **Mechanism:** Exploit price discrepancies for the same coin across exchanges or countries (e.g., the historic South Korea "Kimchi premium" of the same coin priced higher in KRW-denominated exchanges), or triangular arbitrage across three trading pairs on one exchange (e.g., BTC/USD, LTC/USD, BTC/LTC).
- **Claimed performance:** Makarov & Schoar, "Trading and Arbitrage in Cryptocurrency Markets" (*Journal of Financial Economics*, 2020) documented **historic** cross-country premia up to 40% (Korea) persisting hours-to-weeks, with a combined **$1.275B** notional opportunity across US/Korea/Japan/Europe in Nov 2017–Feb 2018. Separately, a high-frequency triangular-arb study on Binance (BTC/LTC/USD) found 4,879 opportunities but only **$12.43–$17.73 total tradable profit for an entire week**.
- **Gross/net of fees:** The triangular-arb figure is already net of realistic size/slippage constraints (that's why it shrank from thousands of "opportunities" to ~$15/week).
- **Kill factors — both explicitly out of scope for this mission:** the cross-country premium is now largely closed to Western retail by capital controls and KYC/won on/off-ramp restrictions (it was never really accessible to a Malaysia-based trader even at its 2017–2018 peak); triangular arb is real but requires the sub-second execution speed and colocation this mission explicitly excludes, and at ~$15/week per pair it's not worth the infrastructure even if latency weren't a constraint.

## What NOT to burn tonight's time on
Perpetual funding-rate carry (#8) is confirmed decayed by the source's own numbers — only worth a 15-minute sanity check of current funding rates, not a full rebuild. Cross-venue/country arb (#14) and listing drift (#12) fail this mission's own retail-latency and capacity screens on paper already — don't build anything for them. The cointegration pairs-trading claims (#11) span a credibility range from a real Emerald journal to a pay-to-publish journal; if pursued, anchor only to the Emerald Sharpe-1.53 figure and expect material decay from a 2015-2018-era sample.
